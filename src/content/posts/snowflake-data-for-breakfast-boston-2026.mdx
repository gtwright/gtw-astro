## Keynote: the three-pillar framework

<!--
Speakers: Nick Pereira (Senior District Manager, Strategic Health & Life Sciences),
Brendan Farley (Solutions Engineering, Enterprise)

OPENING — AI urgency framing:
- Pereira opened with a framing about our moment in time: "our little sliver of existence
  aligns with one of the most disruptive technological advances the world may ever see"
  (PULL QUOTE — may use)
- Argued there will be no catching up if you fall behind — unlike previous technology waves,
  the generational drop-off will be permanent
- This resonates. We're making sure we have a strong position on AI at our organization.
  Not because it's trendy, but because the cost of waiting is higher than the cost of learning.

THREE PILLARS:
1. Unified data foundation — structured, semi-structured, unstructured in one governed environment.
   Iceberg/Delta interoperability means not all data needs to live in Snowflake natively.
2. Business logic and context — semantic layer of rules, KPIs, business definitions. The "brain"
   that turns generic AI into enterprise-specific AI. Without it, 95% of AI pilots fail (MIT).
3. AI embedded in workflows — agents deployed where people already work (Slack, Teams, Streamlit,
   CLI). ROI comes from embedding, not from standalone tools.

STATS:
- AI at maturity could generate $4.4T in added business value/year (McKinsey) — equivalent to
  Japan's GDP
- 88% of organizations struggling to scale AI (IDC)
- 13,000+ Snowflake customers, approaching 70% using weekly
- 2025: more features released than in Snowflake's first 13 years combined

DEMOS:
- Snowflake Intelligence: natural language querying across structured and unstructured data,
  cross-domain (sales, marketing, HR, finance queryable from one agent), RBAC flowing through,
  SQL visible and inspectable, charts configurable, conversation sharing (new). Available at
  ai.snowflake.com — no need to log into Snowflake directly. Orchestration model selectable:
  Claude, Gemini, others, or Auto mode.
- Iceberg interoperability: connected to AWS Glue catalog, created external Iceberg tables.
  Previous 4x performance penalty for external tables now eliminated — near-parity with native.
  Governance (masking policies, tagging) works identically on Iceberg tables.
  Dynamic tables on Iceberg with refresh as frequent as 60 seconds.
- Cortex Code: Snowflake's native coding agent (comparable to Claude Code, Cursor). Available in
  UI and CLI. Can build dbt projects, create Streamlit apps, do governance/data quality work,
  explain existing queries.
- Semantic views: native semantic object in Snowflake. Auto-generated from table metadata in
  ~5-10 seconds. Can import from Tableau, Power BI, dbt semantic layers (interoperable).
  Supports verified queries (pre-defined SQL for known question patterns). Monitoring for cost,
  performance, accuracy.

KEY DEMO FLOW: connect Iceberg data → build dynamic pipeline → create semantic view → add to
agent → serve in Snowflake Intelligence. Claimed ~4-5 minutes end-to-end.

SEMANTIC LAYER ACCURACY: showed a bar chart — dramatic accuracy improvement when AI queries run
against a semantic layer vs. raw database. Green bars (raw) vs. blue bars (with semantic layer).
This is the visual evidence for why Pillar 2 matters.

My take (brief): the three-pillar framing resonated. Most orgs (mine included) have Pillar 1
largely built. Pillar 2 — the semantic layer — is the actual gap. Pillar 3 gets all the

## Customer story: Agelon Health

<!--
Speaker: Alex (data/engineering lead at Agelon Health)

The standout session of the morning.

COMPANY CONTEXT:
- Agelon works with primary care groups taking financial risk for Medicare patients
- Revenue is per-capita (~$1,000/month/patient), risk-adjusted by CMS based on disease profile
- If a patient has an undocumented condition, the provider isn't compensated for actual cost of care
  AND the patient may not be receiving guideline-directed therapy
- Total member base: 500,000-600,000

USE CASE — undocumented diagnosis identification:
- Heart failure example: stage B heart failure (asymptomatic) — patients don't have obvious symptoms
  but structural abnormalities are visible on echocardiogram. If caught early and patient gets on
  guideline-directed medical therapy, can extend life 7-10 years. Often missed because no single
  specialist thinks to look for it.
- Metastatic cancer example: oncologist codes "lung cancer" and "liver cancer" separately but
  doesn't code the metastasis itself — no clinical reason to, but CMS needs it for risk adjustment.
  A PET scan confirming spread is the diagnostic evidence.

SCALE:
- Pilot: 17,000 patients (Nov-Dec 2025), focused on metastatic cancer
- Current: 120,000 patients/week in production
- Target: all 500K-600K members across all covered diseases (heart failure, kidney disease,
  diabetes, cancer)
- Previously: offshore physician team reviewing charts at $5-10/chart

ARCHITECTURE — all in Snowflake:
- Data layer: claims, labs, electronic health records, tens of millions of clinical notes
- Charter/summary AI: document processing, patient chart summarization
- Clinical gate: 5-6 step prompt chain — one agent reads/summarizes, another validates
  (grounding, accuracy, guideline compliance)
- Output: structured "suspects" with evidence citations, presented to PCPs for review
- Orchestration: Snowflake Tasks + dbt projects, deployed via Jenkins from Bitbucket
- No vector database, no external infrastructure
- "Just SQL all the way down" (PULL QUOTE — use)
- "Our DevOps guy wants to get involved in this project, and I just keep telling him it's not
  as sexy as you think." (PULL QUOTE — use)
- "I'm just excited to work on something that just works." (on choosing Snowflake over AWS infra)

WHY SNOWFLAKE FIRST:
- They started experimenting in the AWS world — standing up RAG-based servers, vector databases.
  Too many moving pieces, too much data movement.
- Snowflake PS provided one engineer for 4-5 months to help get started. Data was already there.
  They just needed to write prompts.
- The advantage: no data movement, no infrastructure management, existing governance applies

WHAT'S STILL OUTSIDE SNOWFLAKE:
- OCR for scanned documents (7%+ of their documents aren't converted to text)
- CI/CD: Jenkins deploying from Bitbucket
- dbt projects in Snowflake are relatively new feature — they historically ran on Airflow

MODEL SELECTION:
- Abandoned fine-tuning after initial experiments — too much engineering overhead, models moving
  too fast, couldn't keep up with frontier model improvements
- Claude preferred over OpenAI for coherence and physician-facing output quality
- Stepped down to Sonnet 4 for some steps, Llama 4 Maverick for largest summarization step
  (1/10th the cost)
- Snowflake's input/output token pricing split was significant for cost optimization
- Key takeaway: "The models are moving too fast for fine-tuning." (USE — captures a decision
  many teams are grappling with right now)

COST TRAJECTORY:
- Started at ~$10/patient chart
- Current: under $1/patient
- Three levers: (1) filtering irrelevant documents before processing, (2) model selection per
  step, (3) Snowflake's differentiated token pricing

EVALUATION:
- LLM-as-judge for automated evaluation
- Head-to-head comparison of outputs from different models
- Human physician feedback loop — providers rating memo quality
- Disease-specific criteria (echocardiogram evidence for heart failure, PET scan for metastasis)
- Incoming provider feedback says these memos are better than both offshore physician reviews
  and previous automated approaches

BUSINESS IMPACT:
- Pilot: ~$1M in revenue recovery from cancer patients alone
- Single metastatic cancer patient = ~$1,000+/month in additional risk-adjusted revenue
- Clinical impact: patients get guideline-directed therapy earlier
- Replacing $5-10/chart offshore review with AI at <$1/chart

My take (brief): this mirrors our own experience. We jumped into Snowflake because everything
just worked — DLT loads, dbt transforms, the warehouse runs. Now as the system matures, we're
thinking about where AWS capabilities complement Snowflake. Snowflake and AWS play nicely
together, and that's a genuine advantage. We're not picking a lane — we're benefiting from two
industry-leading partners. The discipline is in not also spreading into Azure, Google Cloud, and
Databricks. Narrowing focus without narrowing to one.
-->
