## Jane Goodall Institute: intelligent document processing

The standout session of the day for me, and the one most directly relevant to work we're doing at the BSO. Dr. Lilian Pintea had appeared in the morning keynote at a high level, but this afternoon build session with Sri Elaprolu from the AWS Generative AI Innovation Center went deep on the technical implementation.

Sri walked through how the Jane Goodall Institute is using intelligent document processing to unlock decades of field research notes from Gombe. The challenges were immediately familiar: handwriting that varies across researchers (some nearly illegible even to humans), domain-specific shorthands, ambiguous timestamps with no AM/PM notation, and — the biggest of all — translation from Swahili and the Gombe dialect into English, with no large language model specifically trained on Swahili.

Their approach leaned on three prompting techniques rather than retraining models: in-context learning to teach the model new tasks through examples and instructions, visual language models (Qwen V7 on Amazon Bedrock) that process images paired with text for handwriting recognition, and few-shot prompting to handle domain logic like timestamp interpretation. The architecture is straightforward — scanned documents in, structured JSON out (date, team observed, timestamps, extracted notes, translation), stored in DynamoDB. Processing time ranges from ten seconds to about a minute per document.

What made this more than a technical demo was the human-in-the-loop design. The UI shows the original field notes on the left and extracted data on the right, with confidence scores for each translation. Reviewers can edit, confirm, or deny before anything enters the database. Field researchers who previously waited weeks or months for translated data from Gombe now get it in minutes.
