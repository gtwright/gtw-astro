# The Promise of AI Productivity

## Overview
A UC Berkeley study found that AI tools deployed without structure led to work
intensification, not efficiency gains. At the BSO, we're in early days of an AI
pilot taking a different approach — deploying within defined roles and specific
workstreams, guided by a framework for deciding where AI belongs and where it
doesn't. The study sharpens that framework. A shorter, reactive post that
establishes the framework publicly before the longer "Machines for the Machine
Work" flagship essay.

## Audience
Dual audience. Primary: peer digital/technology leaders designing AI rollouts.
Secondary: CEOs and EDs who expect AI to deliver efficiency gains and need to
understand what that actually requires. Register: reflective, strategic.
Recinos (mission-first, organizational specificity) + Dharmesh (figuring-it-out
posture, honest about being early).

## Outline

### The efficiency expectation
Executives across the performing arts sector — and nonprofits broadly — are
asking the same question: how will AI make us more efficient? The expectation
is reasonable: free up staff time, do more with less, redirect capacity toward
mission. Technology leaders are tasked with delivering on that expectation. The
answer isn't as straightforward as the promise suggests.

### What the research found
Cite HBR study directly: Ranganathan and Ye (UC Berkeley), eight months
embedded in a ~200-person tech company. AI tools were offered but not mandated
or structured. Three findings:

1. **Task expansion** — people took on work outside their roles. Product
   managers writing code, researchers handling engineering tasks. The
   researchers: "Workers described this approach as 'just trying things' with
   AI. These experimental interactions accumulated into meaningful job scope
   expansion."

2. **Blurred boundaries** — natural pauses disappeared. Work became "less
   bounded and more ambient — something that could always be advanced a little
   further."

3. **Increased multitasking** — multiple simultaneous threads, constant
   attention-switching despite feeling productive.

The engineer's quote captures it: "You had thought that maybe, oh, because you
could be more productive with AI, then you save some time, you can work less.
But then really, you don't work less. You just work the same amount or even
more."

The researchers' conclusion: a self-reinforcing cycle. Faster task completion
raised speed expectations, which increased AI reliance, which broadened work
scope, which expanded workload.

### The nonprofit version of this risk
Rasheeda Childress at the Chronicle of Philanthropy applied the findings to
nonprofits, where burnout is already a persistent structural problem and the
temptation is to use AI to "add to one's work force without increasing
personnel expenses." The $20/month framing is vivid: an AI tool that makes
someone 20% more productive is equivalent to an extra day of work. The question
is what happens with that day.

Nathan Chappell (fundraising.AI) on the nonprofit-specific danger: the
"productivity paradox" — increased output but higher rates of burnout — is
"one of the things I worry about the most with AI in our sector."

Make it concrete for performing arts: you give your marketing team AI and
suddenly they're drafting grant proposals, building reports, doing graphic
design — not because anyone asked, but because the friction dropped. The scope
creep is the specific danger: when anyone can do more, the definition of their
role quietly expands. The problem isn't AI. The problem is deploying AI without
deciding in advance where the reclaimed time goes.

### Safeguarding the time
Chappell's framing aligns with how we're thinking about it: "If I can use a
tool to gain 20 percent efficiency, my team is to then safeguard that 20
percent time to do things that are going to be really additive. And that means
things that are inherently human."

This is the key move: the efficiency gain is real, but it doesn't deliver
itself. Someone has to decide — before deployment — where the reclaimed time
goes. Not "use AI however you want and see what happens." Not "wait until we
have every policy mapped." A middle path: deploy within defined roles and
specific workstreams, with a clear framework for which work is best suited for
a machine, which involves genuine tradeoffs, and which should stay
human-centered.

### Where we are at the BSO
Early days of a pilot. Around a dozen users testing specific use cases, with
plans to enroll twice as many in the coming weeks. The approach: specific
workstreams within defined roles. We're deciding before deployment which tasks
are candidates for AI and which aren't — not as a blanket policy, but
workstream by workstream, with the people who do the work.

The study's findings sharpen that approach. The scope creep examples are exactly
the kind of work that needs an intentional decision before AI takes it on —
not because AI can't do it, but because letting it happen by default is how you
get intensification instead of efficiency. The researchers' recommendation for
"human grounding" — maintaining dialogue and connection — reinforces that the
goal isn't to automate more. It's to shift what kind of work people do.

### What we're watching for
The pilot is weeks old, not months. The framework is a hypothesis, not a proven
methodology. And the boundary question is genuinely open: too tight and there's
less room for the creative experimentation that produces the best discoveries
about where AI actually helps. Too broad and you get exactly what the study
describes — scope creep, ambient work, intensification dressed up as
productivity. Research like this is a useful input for calibrating where that
line should be. We're paying attention to whether the boundaries hold when more
people have access, whether the reclaimed time goes where we intended, and
whether the structure leaves enough room for the unexpected wins. Still
figuring it out.

## Ending
Open — honest about being early, watching, will revisit. The researchers'
closing line is the right note to end near: "Without intention, AI makes it
easier to do more — but harder to stop." The promise of AI productivity is
real, but it requires intention. The efficiency comes from the decisions you
make about deployment, not from the tool.
